Wild Veracruz

Five remarkable wild species share the urban coastline of Veracruz–Boca del Río with us. It's time we truly see them.

📍 Zona Conurbada Veracruz · Boca del Río · Mexico

The metropolitan area of Veracruz–Boca del Río is one of Mexico's most ecologically rich urban zones. Bordered by the Gulf of Mexico, crisscrossed by rivers and lagoons, and neighbored by protected reefs, it shelters an astonishing diversity of wildlife — most of it invisible to the daily passerby. These five species live among us. Each one is struggling.

01

Species 01 · Aquatic Mammal

West Indian Manatee

Trichechus manatus

🌊 Coastal lagoons & river mouths

The manatee is one of the Gulf of Mexico's most iconic gentle giants. In the Veracruz region, manatees inhabit the calm, warm, shallow waters of lagoons like Laguna de Tamiahua and the estuaries near Boca del Río. They feed on submerged aquatic vegetation and can stay underwater for up to 20 minutes. Listed as Vulnerable by the IUCN and protected under Mexico's NOM-059-SEMARNAT-2010, their populations have declined dramatically due to human activity along the Gulf coast.

© Wikimedia Commons / Public Domain

⚠ Threats in our region

Manatees in Veracruz face serious threats from boat propeller strikes — the leading cause of injury and death — as watercraft traffic in the lagoons has grown sharply. Pollution from urban runoff and industrial discharge degrades the seagrass beds they depend on. Entanglement in fishing nets and habitat loss from coastal development further reduce their numbers.

★ Fun Facts

01

Elephant cousins

Despite living in water, manatees are most closely related to elephants. Their common ancestor roamed land some 60 million years ago — they even share similar toenails and thick skin.

02

Nonstop chewing

A manatee eats up to 10% of its body weight in seagrass every single day. They spend 6–8 hours grazing, earning them the nickname "sea cows."

03

Mermaids of legend

Sailors who spotted manatees for the first time reported seeing mermaids. Christopher Columbus himself wrote about them in his ship's log during his first voyage to the Americas.

Species 02 · Reef Builder

Elkhorn Coral

✏ Research by Andrew Bell

Acropora palmata

🐠 Parque Nacional Sistema Arrecifal Veracruzano

The Elkhorn coral lives in the coral reefs of Veracruz, mainly within the Parque Nacional Sistema Arrecifal Veracruzano, where the ecosystem connects with nearby mangroves, coastal dunes, and even urban areas. On a local map of the Veracruz–Boca del Río zone, it would be found offshore in front of the port and hotel zone. Corals get most of their energy from symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) that use sunlight to produce nutrients — and they also capture tiny zooplankton from surrounding water to supplement their diet.

© NOAA / Wikimedia Commons

⚠ Threats in our region

In Veracruz, coral reefs face local urban dangers such as plastic pollution in the harbor, sewage and runoff from the city, sediment from construction, port activity, and boat traffic. Under Mexico's NOM-059-SEMARNAT-2010, Acropora palmata and Acropora cervicornis are listed under Special Protection. ⚠ Not-so-fun fact: almost all corals that look stunningly bright and shiny are actually bleaching under stress — a sign they may be dying if conditions do not improve.

★ Fun Facts

01

Animals, not plants

Corals are animals. Each colony is made of hundreds of polyps — tiny creatures with mouths surrounded by tentacles that sting and capture microscopic prey to feed the colony.

02

Hard vs. soft

Hard corals have rigid calcium carbonate skeletons shaped like brains, staghorns, and fans. Soft corals lack a solid skeleton and move gently with the current, looking more like plants or shrubs.

03

Ocean's pharmacies

Coral reefs have yielded compounds used in treatments for cancer, arthritis, and HIV. Scientists estimate that reefs may hold the key to thousands of undiscovered medicines.

Species 03 · Urban Wildlife

Virginia Opossum

✏ Research by Project Sandbox

Didelphis virginiana · "Tlacuache"

🌳 Urban green spaces & stream banks

The tlacuache is Mexico's only native marsupial — a cousin of kangaroos and koalas, carrying its babies in a small pouch on its belly. You have probably seen them running across a wall at night. They are nocturnal, peaceful, and solitary. In the Veracruz–Boca del Río metropolitan area, their natural habitats — mangroves, riparian corridors, and urban green spaces — are constantly shrinking as the city grows. Far from being pests, tlacuaches work quietly every night to control insects, rodents, and ticks in our ecosystem.

© Wikimedia Commons

⚠ Threats in our region

Urban growth in Veracruz and Boca del Río destroys their homes, forcing them further into the city. Traffic accidents are the #1 cause of death for adult tlacuaches — they are slow and have poor eyesight. Pet attacks from outdoor dogs and cats are a constant threat. Perhaps worst: human ignorance — many people mistake them for "giant rats" that carry diseases, leading them to be intentionally hurt or poisoned out of fear.

★ Fun Facts

01

Nature's superheroes

Their low body temperature makes tlacuaches practically immune to rabies. They are also naturally immune to the venom of local snakes — like rattlesnakes — and will even eat them.

02

The hanging myth

Adult tlacuaches are too heavy to sleep hanging from their tails. They use their prehensile tails to grip branches — but only babies can truly hang from them, and only for a few seconds.

03

"Playing dead" is a coma

When terrified, they don't just act dead — extreme fear triggers a real involuntary coma called thanatosis. Their bodies stiffen and release a rotting smell to convince predators they are already dead.

Species 04 · Marine Reptile

Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle

Lepidochelys kempii

🏖 Gulf beaches & offshore waters

The Gulf of Mexico coast near Veracruz is a critical migratory corridor and nesting zone for the Kemp's Ridley — the world's most endangered sea turtle. Female turtles return to the same beaches where they were born to nest, sometimes traveling thousands of kilometers across the open ocean. Beaches near Antón Lizardo and the southern Veracruz coast serve as active nesting sites. These ancient reptiles have existed for over 100 million years — long before humans, long before this city.

© NOAA / Wikimedia Commons

⚠ Threats in our region

Nesting beaches near Veracruz are increasingly compromised by light pollution from urban development, which disorients hatchlings that use moonlight to find the ocean. Beach recreation and unregulated tourism disturb nesting females. Bycatch in shrimp trawling nets kills thousands of sea turtles annually in Gulf waters. Plastic pollution and rising sea temperatures — which skew the gender of hatchlings — add further pressure. The Kemp's Ridley is listed as Critically Endangered.

★ Fun Facts

01

Temperature = gender

Sea turtle gender is not determined by genetics, but by nest temperature. Warmer nests produce more females; cooler nests produce more males. Climate change is skewing this ratio dangerously.

02

Ancient navigators

Sea turtles use Earth's magnetic field as a GPS system. They can navigate thousands of kilometers of open ocean and return to the exact beach where they were born — sometimes 30 years later.

03

Crying salt

Sea turtles appear to cry on the beach. They are actually secreting excess salt through special glands near their eyes — a water-saving adaptation to life in the sea.

Species 05 · Crustacean

Blue Land Crab

Cardisoma guanhumi · "Cangrejo azul"

🌿 Mangroves, estuaries & coastal forest

The blue land crab (Cardisoma guanhumi) — known locally as "cangrejo azul" — is a large terrestrial crab that lives in burrows in the humid coastal forests, mangrove edges, and estuaries of the Veracruz region. Unlike the sea-dwelling jaiba (Callinectes sapidus), this species spends most of its life on land, returning to the sea only to release its larvae. It is found in areas around Laguna de Mandinga, the Boca del Río coastline, and nearby mangrove systems. It is both a keystone species and a culturally significant animal in coastal Veracruz communities.

© Wikimedia Commons

⚠ Threats in our region

Overharvesting is the most immediate threat — blue land crabs are collected for food before reaching reproductive maturity, destabilizing local populations. The destruction of mangrove forests and coastal vegetation — their primary habitat — has accelerated as the urban area expands. Pollution from urban and agricultural runoff contaminates the burrow zones where they live, and they are highly vulnerable to pesticides used in nearby fields and gardens.

★ Fun Facts

01

Land dweller, sea breeder

Despite living on land, female blue land crabs must travel to the ocean to release their eggs into the sea. The larvae develop in saltwater before the juveniles return to land to burrow.

02

Growth by molting

To grow, blue land crabs must shed their entire hard shell. For several hours after molting they are completely soft and defenseless, hiding deep in their burrows to survive this vulnerable moment.

03

Ecosystem engineers

Their digging aerates the soil and improves drainage in coastal forests. They also consume leaf litter and organic matter, acting as natural recyclers in the mangrove ecosystem.

Our Responsibility

We don't have to choose between the city and the wild.

The manatee in the lagoon, the coral off the port, the tlacuache in your backyard, the turtle on the beach, the blue land crab in the mangrove — none of them migrated to our territory. We built our city into theirs. The question was never whether humans and wildlife can coexist. The question is whether we are willing to.

Veracruz has a rare gift: extraordinary biodiversity within an urban setting. Protecting it doesn't require leaving the city. It requires changing how we live inside it — how we dispose of waste, how we treat the animals we encounter, how we build, how we fish, and how we teach the next generation to see the world around them.

Every action — or inaction — is a choice.

🚤

Slow down on the water

Reduce boat speed in lagoon zones. Propeller strikes are the leading cause of manatee injuries in our region.

🏖️

Respect the beach at night

Dim lights near nesting beaches. Keep dogs leashed. Never drive on sand during turtle nesting season.

🌿

Protect the tlacuache

Do not harm them — let them be. Keep pets inside at night. Slow down in green zones. Spread the word: tlacuaches are harmless and helpful.

🪸

Don't touch the reef

Never step on or collect coral pieces or shells. Report illegal extraction or pollution to PROFEPA: 800-PROFEPA (800-776-3372).

🦀

Protect the mangroves

Mangroves are the blue crab's home. Oppose their destruction. Dispose of plastics and chemicals properly — they reach these ecosystems.

📢

Raise your voice

Support conservation organizations, demand environmental reviews for coastal projects, and share what you've learned today.
